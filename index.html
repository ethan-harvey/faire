Ye Olde Faire

Every year, performers, vendors, re-enactors, and eccentrics converge on this run-down park for a celebration of dubious history, feats of strength and skill, fairy-tale nonsense, and terrible puns. Jousters knock each other flying from their horses. Siege weapons fling pumpkins into the air. Hastily made stages wobble beneath duels and belly dancers. Knife vendors flirt with everyone who walks by. The people in garb hand-woven from period materials grouse about the people running around with plastic fairy wings. It’s a chaotic, colorful mess, and you do it every year because at least some part of you loves it.

But the real drama happens after hours, when you and the rest of the staff are free to argue, gossip, hook up, and plot revenge for that prank they played on you three years ago. You have a few weeks to cram in a year’s worth of shenanigans with these people, so make the most of it.

Introduction

This is an add-on for playing Unincorporated as the staff and volunteers at a Renaissance fair instead of the default small town. You’ll need the original game, which you can download at ethanharvey.itch.io/unincorporated. You can replace the original Questions, Plots, and Examples with the ones in this playbook or combine them for more variety. It’s designed to play over one or two sessions. If you want to play a longer campaign, you’ll probably need to add Plots from the original game, or make up your own.

Some tips for this setting:

  • Characters could be any mix of traveling vendors; professional performers; locals from schools, museums, bands, and theater and dance clubs; LARP and Society for Creative Anachronism groups; and municipal employees.
  • If you’re having trouble coming up with a name for an attraction, business, or performer, you can never go wrong with dad jokes.
  • Don’t worry about historical accuracy. While some groups do try to educate visitors about life in historical periods and you’ll find that one guy who’ll show you how to make your own loom from period-accurate materials, Renaissance fairs are mostly about light-hearted depictions of “the past.” Combine Monty Python and the Holy Grail and Disney’s Robin Hood cartoon and you’ve got the right vibe.

Questions

People

  • Who criticizes your historically inaccurate garb?
  • Who tries to set you up with their annoying relative?
  • Who got hurt because of your careless mistake?
  • Who could get you banned from the Faire if they wanted to?
  • Who are you sure only beat you by cheating?
  • Who never remembers your name, though you know theirs?

Places

  • Where do you avoid going because of who you might run into?
  • Where do the most obnoxious visitors hang out?
  • Where did you get caught kissing someone you shouldn’t have?
  • Where have you been unfairly kicked out of?
  • Where do you go when you want peace and quiet?
  • Where is the worst place to get stuck working?

Things

  • What do visitors keep trying to steal?
  • What are you not allowed to bring out of your tent/vehicle?
  • What handmade gift did your ex give you?
  • What do you keep having to fix?
  • What irreplaceable item have you misplaced?
  • What did you find that was obviously hidden on purpose?

Plots

  • Person 1 has challenged you to a boffer duel at Place. Winner gets Thing and, more importantly, bragging rights. You can win if you cheat, but you can’t let your (boss|relative|crush), Person 2, find out.
  • Person 1, a stickler for historical accuracy, has banned phones from Place. You need to be there, but you’re waiting for an important call from Person 2.
  • Person 1 broke Thing, but you took the blame. Every day since then, Person 2 has made sure you get stuck being the human archery target. Person 1 will admit it was their fault, if you do them a favor…
  • You didn’t recognize your (ex|relative|frenemy), Person 1 in their plague doctor costume, watching you make a complete fool of yourself last night at Place. They recorded it all. You just know they’re going to show it to Person 2, your (crush/mentor/boss).
  • Your hasty repair of Thing or Place is failing. Someone could get hurt if you don’t fix it soon, but you need to get everyone out of there first, and Person won’t shut it down.
  • Person 1 got their fortune told by Person 2, who told them something terrible would happen to them while they were alone. Now Person 1 is following you everywhere.
  • You haven’t seen Person 1 since last year’s Faire, but you haven’t been able to get them out of your mind. This year they’re working at Place. Unfortunately, Person 2 is also flirting with them.
  • Some regular visitors always heckle Person 1 at their show. Person 1 doesn’t care, but they’ve finally talked Person 2 into performing with them. They want you to keep the hecklers away today. But you need to be across the park at Place.
  • Person entrusted Thing to you while they went into town. But you’ve lost it. You last saw it at Place.
  • Hiding Thing in someone’s vehicle, tent, or booth has become a tradition. You’ve bet Person 1 you can sneak it into Place for Person 2 to find. Good luck.
  • Person, whom you want to impress, needs your help at Place. Unfortunately, you have a phobia of Things, and there will be lots of them there.

Note to Players

This is a beta version. The basic system has been extensively playtested, but this particular setting has only been played a few times.

I’d love to hear feedback. Especially if you have a lot of experience with Renaissance fairs and think I got something wrong, or if you have no experience with Renaissance fairs and were confused about something.

You can leave a comment on the project page at ethanharvey.itch.io/faire or message me on Twitter (@byEthanHarvey) or Mastodon (@cavedueller@dice.camp).
